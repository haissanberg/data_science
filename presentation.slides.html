Students’ Academic Performance Analysis¶

Foundations of Data Science and Lab — A.Y. 2024/2025 Hassan Ahmed

Dataset: Students Performance in Exams (Kaggle)

Motivation¶

Goal: understand which factors are associated with students’ exam performance.

Research questions:

  • How are math scores distributed?
  • Are there differences by gender?
  • Does test preparation help?
  • Are subjects related (math, reading, writing)?
  • Can we predict math score from other variables?

Dataset¶

Source: Kaggle — Students Performance in Exams

Rows: 1000 students Variables:

  • Gender
  • Race/ethnicity (group A–E)
  • Parental level of education
  • Lunch (standard vs free/reduced)
  • Test preparation course (none vs completed)
  • Scores: math, reading, writing (0–100)

Target for modelling: math_score

Data preparation¶

  • Renamed columns for readability (e.g., math score → math_score)
  • Checked missing values (none in this dataset)
  • Created an additional feature:
    • average_score = (math + reading + writing) / 3

Why: easier analysis, cleaner plots, and modelling.

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)¶

We explore distributions and group differences, and we inspect relationships among subjects.

Distribution of Math Scores¶

What we see:

  • Most students score around the middle/high range.
  • Few very low scores appear as outliers.

Math Score by Gender¶

Interpretation:

  • Compare medians and spread between genders.
  • Outliers indicate a few students with very low scores.

¶

Effect of Test Preparation

Interpretation:

  • Students who completed the course tend to score higher in math.
  • The distribution shifts upward compared to “none”.

Correlation Between Subjects¶

Key message:

  • Reading and writing are strongly correlated.
  • Math is positively related to the other subjects too.

Conclusions¶

Main findings:

  • Math scores show a roughly bell-shaped distribution with few low-score outliers.
  • Test preparation is associated with higher math performance.
  • Reading and writing are strongly related; all subjects are positively correlated.
  • A simple linear regression can predict math score reasonably well.

Limitations:

  • Observational data → correlation, not causation.
  • Few features → missing factors like study time, school context, etc.
